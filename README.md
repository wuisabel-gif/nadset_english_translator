# The Clockwork Translator

A horrorshow little machine for viddying the words of Nadsat and turning them back again, O my brothers. Translate between plain old veck-talk and the Russian-laced teen slang Anthony Burgess dreamt up for *A Clockwork Orange*, complete with a 242-word lexicon and etymological notes.

> Nadsat is not some grand language of its own, O my brothers. It's English underneath, with the odd slovo swapped in. Just as Alex tells it, this translator changes the words it knows and leaves the rest to govoreet for themselves.

## A Note from Isabella

One night, O my brothers, I had the most horrorshow dream.

I found myself wandering the streets of a strange city where the milkbars glowed white, the chellovecks spoke in half-Russian riddles, and nobody seemed interested in explaining what any of it meant. Everywhere I turned, there were slovos I couldn't understand.

"Come with us, little devotchka."
"What in the world is a devotchka?" I asked.
Nobody answered. A droog laughed. Another called me a veck. Someone threatened my yarbles.

At which point I decided a dictionary would be extremely useful.

When I woke, the dream was gone — but it left something behind, like a reel that had fallen out of the novel:

> **ALEX** *(voice-over)*: There was this young devotchka named Isabella Wu who dropped into our little world one night.
>
> **ISABELLA**: Excuse me — what does *droog* mean?
>
> **DIM**: Ha!

> **GEORGIE**: She doesn't know what *droog* means.
>
> **ISABELLA**: Should I?
>
> **ALEX**: Poor innocent chelloveck.
>
> **ISABELLA**: Fine. I'll make a translator.
>
> **DIM**: A what?

> **ISABELLA**: A translator.
>
> **GEORGIE**: For Nadsat?
>
> **ISABELLA**: For Nadsat.

And that, O my brothers, was the beginning of the whole story.

## Horrorshow Features

- **Live two-way translation** — English → Nadsat and Nadsat → English as you type.
- **Hover any slovo** to see its meaning and linguistic root (for example, *droog* ← Russian друг / *drug*, meaning "friend").
- **Juvenile Mode** — favors Alex's childish coinages and baby-talk expressions (*eggiweg*, *guttiwuts*, *skolliwoll*).
- **Shuffle & Reroll** — spins the wheel among multiple Nadsat equivalents when they exist.
- **Copy or Share** — grab the translated text or generate a link that restores the exact translation later.
- **Search the Lexicon** by language of origin, meaning, or Nadsat term.

## The Lexicon

Inside you'll find 242 carefully collected entries, cross-checked against multiple glossaries and reference sources.

Most of the slovos (187 of them) come from Russian roots, while the rest drift in from German, French, Malay, Romani, Cockney rhyming slang, and Burgess's own invented juvenile language.

For the full catalogue, viddy [`vocabulary.md`](vocabulary.md).

## Limitations, O My Brothers

This machine is clever, but not quite as clever as old Burgess.

- Verb endings follow simplified rules, so some forms may differ from the novel.
- Where several Nadsat words fit the same English meaning, the translator chooses one (or shuffles between them).
- Some capitalization and grammar nuances are lost in translation.
- Nadsat itself was never a complete language, so certain expressions must remain ordinary English.
